## The pilot trap

Most large enterprises run dozens of AI pilots simultaneously. Innovation labs are buzzing, proof-of-concepts get demoed to excited stakeholders, and data science teams deliver impressive results in controlled environments.

But here's the uncomfortable truth:

The challenge is not ideation — it's **industrialization**. The gap between a successful pilot and a production-grade AI system is vast, and it's where most organizations falter.

### Why pilots fail to scale

The reasons are rarely technical. They're organizational, cultural, and architectural:

- **No MLOps infrastructure** — Models built in Jupyter notebooks can't deploy, monitor, or retrain at scale without proper MLOps pipelines.
- **Siloed data** — Pilot teams often work with curated datasets. Production requires access to enterprise-wide, real-time data across business units.
- **No executive sponsorship** — AI initiatives that lack C-suite backing get deprioritized when budgets tighten.
- **Skills gap** — Data scientists who build great models aren't always the ones who can productionize them. ML engineering is a distinct discipline.
- **Technical debt** — Quick wins in pilots create code and architecture that can't scale, leading to complete rewrites for production.

## Governance and risk

Enterprise AI at scale requires robust governance. This is especially true in regulated industries like banking and healthcare, where model decisions have profound consequences.

Key governance requirements:

- **Model Risk Management (MRM)** — Every production model needs documented validation, testing, and ongoing monitoring aligned with SR 11-7 and similar regulatory frameworks.
- **Explainability** — Stakeholders, regulators, and customers need to understand how AI makes decisions. Black-box models are increasingly unacceptable.
- **Bias monitoring** — AI models can perpetuate or amplify biases in training data. Continuous fairness monitoring isn't optional — it's a legal and ethical requirement.
- **Audit trails** — Maintain complete lineage from data source to model prediction, including data transformations, feature engineering, and model versioning.
